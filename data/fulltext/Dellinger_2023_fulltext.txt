Abstract
Background and Aims: The prevalence of NAFLD is increasing globally
and on a path to becoming the most frequent cause of chronic liver disease.
Strategies for the prevention and treatment of NAFLD are urgently needed.
Approach and Results: A 6‐month prospective, randomized, double‐blind,
placebo‐controlled clinical trial was conducted to assess the efficacy of daily
NRPT (commercially known as Basis, a combination of nicotinamide riboside
and pterostilbene) supplementation in 111 adults with NAFLD. The study con-
sisted of three arms: placebo, recommended daily dose of NRPT (NRPT 1×),
and a double dose of NRPT (NRPT 2×). NRPT appeared safe and well tol-
erated. At the end of the study, no significant change was seen in the primary
endpoint of hepatic fat fraction with respect to placebo. However, among pre-
specified secondary outcomes, a time‐dependent decrease in the circulating
levels of the liver enzymes alanine aminotransferase (ALT) and gamma‐gluta-
myltransferase (GGT) was observed in the NRPT 1× group, and this decrease
was significant with respect to placebo. Furthermore, a significant decrease in
the circulating levels of the toxic lipid ceramide 14:0 was also observed in the
NRPT 1× group versus placebo, and this decrease was associated with a
decrease in ALT in individuals of this group. A dose‐dependent effect was not
observed with respect to ALT, GGT, or ceramide 14:0 in the NRPT 2× group.
Conclusions: This study demonstrates that NRPT at the recommended dose is
safe and may hold promise in lowering markers of hepatic inflammation in
patients with NAFLD.

INTRODUCTION
NAFLD is closely linked to the epidemics of obesity,
Type
2
diabetes,
and
metabolic
syndrome.
The
prevalence of NAFLD continues to increase and is
becoming the most frequent cause of chronic liver
disease with almost 1 billion people being affected
globally.[1] NAFLD is considered to be the hepatic
manifestation of metabolic syndrome and is character-
ized by abnormally high fat accumulation (steatosis)
and inflammation in the liver. NAFLD can progress to
NASH,
which
is
characterized
by
severe
hepatic
inflammation, fibrosis, and cell death leading to cirrhosis
and HCC.[2] Several serum markers correlate with an
inflammatory response to fat accumulation in NAFLD
and NASH, including alanine aminotransferase (ALT),
aspartate aminotransferase (AST),
gamma‐glutamyl
transferase (GGT), as well as the toxic lipid species
ceramides; and elevated levels of these markers are
associated with poorer prognosis.[3–5] Identifying treat-
ments to address NAFLD and its associated inflamma-
tion is of high clinical interest.
The sirtuins (SIRTs) are NAD+‐dependent protein
deacetylases, which slow aging in many organisms.[6]
These proteins also have many salutary metabolic
effects on various organ systems, including the liver.
Considerable evidence has demonstrated that SIRTs,
most notably SIRT1 and SIRT3, have the ability to
enhance
fat
oxidation
and
prevent
metabolic
disease.[2,7–9] Therefore, strategies to activate SIRT1
and SIRT3 to prevent and/or treat metabolic diseases,
including NAFLD, have started to gain traction.
In rodents, metabolic function in the liver can be
improved by activating SIRT1.[10–14] One approach to
activate SIRT1 and SIRT3 is through the addition of an
NAD+ precursor such as nicotinamide riboside (NR).
Supplementation with NR in mice has been shown to
increase NAD+ levels, as well as increase SIRT1 and
SIRT3 activities, resulting in enhanced oxidative metab-
olism and protection against high‐fat diet–induced
weight gain and metabolic dysfunction.[15] In mouse
models
of
NAFLD,
reduced
hepatic
NAD+
levels
correlate with increases in hepatic weight, lipid content,
and liver damage as evidenced by elevated blood levels
of ALT and AST. Critically, NAD+ repletion through NR
feeding has been shown to reverse these effects.[16]
SIRTs can also be activated by an NAD+‐independ-
ent approach, with the addition of SIRT‐activating
compounds.[17] Pterostilbene (PT), a naturally occurring
polyphenol found in blueberries, is a structural analogue
of
resveratrol
and
an
even
more
potent
SIRT1
activator.[18–20] Notably, PT has been shown to reduce
hepatic steatosis and modify the hepatic fatty acid profile
in obese rats due to increased fatty acid oxidation.[21]
NRPT (commercially known as Basis) is an orally
available combination of NR and PT, compounds shown
to work synergistically.[22] NRPT has been demonstrated to
be safe in humans.[23] In a double‐blind, placebo‐controlled,
randomized trial involving 120 generally healthy adults over
a 2‐month period, NRPT taken daily at the recommended
dose (NRPT 1×) increased NAD+ levels in the blood by
40%, while NRPT at twice the recommended dose (NRPT
2×) increased NAD+ levels by up to 90%.[23] The placebo
group did not show changes in NAD+ levels throughout the
study. Intriguingly, NRPT 1×, but not NRPT 2×, appeared to
significantly reduce serum ALT levels compared to baseline
at both the 30‐day and 60‐day time points, indicating a need
for additional clinical studies to replicate and extend these
findings on NRPT and liver health.[23]
In this study, we investigated the efficacy of NRPT in
111
adult
participants
with
NAFLD
in
a
6‐month,
randomized, double‐blind, placebo‐controlled human trial.
The primary objective of this study was to determine
whether hepatic fat fraction (HFF), as measured by MRI‐
derived proton density fat fraction (MRI‐PDFF) and a
calculated fatty liver index (FLI) score, was reduced by
daily supplementation with NRPT. Prespecified secon-
dary
endpoints
included
the
effects
of
NRPT
on
biomarkers of liver inflammation and assessment of
safety and tolerability in patients with NAFLD.
PARTICIPANTS AND METHODS
Clinical trial
This
clinical
study
(ClinicalTrials.gov
identifier
NCT
03513523) was conducted in accordance with the protocol
and with the consensus ethical principles derived from
international guidelines including the Declaration of Hel-
sinki and the Council for International Organizations of
Medical Sciences International Ethical Guidelines. Appli-
cable International Council for Harmonization good clinical
practice guidelines were also followed. The study protocol
(version 1.1) was approved by Chesapeake Institutional
Review Board (Columbia, MD) on November 16, 2017.
This study was a randomized, double blind, placebo‐
controlled trial carried out at nine clinical sites in the
United States. On‐site monitoring, data management,
and statistical analysis were provided by Nutrasource
(Guelph, ON, Canada). The intervention phase was
26 weeks, with a 14‐day follow‐up period. Participants
were randomized into three arms (1:1:1): placebo,
recommended dietary supplement dose (NRPT 1×), or
double the recommended dose (NRPT 2×). The study
recruited participants from April 2018 and had com-
pleted the in‐human phase of the trial by April 2020.
Participants
Participants were eligible to be enrolled if they were
generally
healthy
adult
males
and
females,
aged
18–70 years, and overweight or obese (body mass index

All participants provided written informed consent to
participate in the study.
Intervention
The investigational product (IP) NRPT (commercially known
as Basis) contained 125 mg NR and 25 mg PT per capsule.
Placebo capsules were indistinguishable from the NRPT
capsules based on appearance and weight. All participants
took four capsules daily. Each capsule contained the
excipients microcrystalline cellulose, silicon dioxide, and
magnesium stearate. Each participant was randomized to
receive one of the three following study interventions daily:
(1) four placebo capsules, (2) 250 mg NR and 50 mg PT
(two NRPT capsules and two placebo capsules), or (3)
500 mg NR and 100 mg PT (four NRPT capsules).
Randomization and blinding
Participants of this study (111 participants) were random-
ized using an online randomization scheme generated
prior to the study start (http://randomization.com) into three
groups, with 37, 41, and 33 participants in the high‐dose,
low‐dose, and placebo groups, respectively (Figure 1A).
The randomization schedule and study product code list
were maintained within the interactive voice/web response
system, which aimed for equal randomization within each
site and across the study. This was a double‐blind,
double‐dummy study; all participants and study personnel
(i.e., sponsor representatives involved in the study,
investigators, study team, data management, participant
evaluators, and any health care professionals or other site
personnel involved in participant management) remained
blinded to each participant's assigned study product
throughout the study.
Clinic visits
Participants attended five in‐clinic visits: one screening
visit (Visit 1, up to 4 weeks prior to the baseline visit), a
baseline/randomization visit (Visit 2, Day 0), two interim
visits at Weeks 6 (Visit 3) and 12 (Visit 4), and one end‐
of‐study visit at Week 26 (Visit 5, Day 180), with
subsequent telephone follow‐up 2 weeks after the last
clinic visit. During the baseline visit, participants under-
went
body
measurements,
provided
fasting
blood
samples for safety endpoints and liver function tests,
and received an MRI‐PDFF assessment, which was
used in determining HFF.[24–26] Starting from Visit 2,
participants took assigned study products once daily in
the morning for 6 months. For interim visits, body
measurements were conducted at Visit 3 and Visit 4,
and blood samples were collected at Visit 4 for liver
function tests. At the end of the study (Visit 5), all
baseline procedures were performed again.
Trial endpoints
The primary endpoint was the within‐individual change of
HFF from baseline comparing each intervention group to
placebo at 6 months. The primary efficacy objective of this
study was to determine if supplementation with NRPT daily
over a 6‐month period would result in a change in liver fat
accumulation and if there was a dose effect of NRPT on
changes from baseline in liver fat content compared to
placebo. A minimum threshold of 15% HFF was required
for inclusion in this study. One previously published trial of
NR on a prediabetic population reported that five out of
eight subjects starting with ~15% HFF or higher had
reduced HFF at the end of the study, while none of the 10
subjects starting with <15% HFF responded.[27] The
primary endpoint was measured using two methods in
the per‐protocol (PP) population. First, MRI‐PDFF imaging
of the liver was used to calculate the HFF. Second, the FLI
was calculated using a standard equation[28]:

Prespecified secondary endpoints included blood
measures of liver function and inflammation including
ALT, AST, alkaline phosphatase (ALP), and GGT.
Other prespecified secondary endpoints included cho-
lesterol, insulin resistance as measured by homeostatic
model assessment‐insulin resistance (HOMA‐IR), β‐cell
function as measured by HOMA‐β, and C‐reactive
protein (CRP), as well as safety and tolerability. In
addition, levels of ceramides in blood were examined as
described in the Supporting Methods. All biomarkers
were measured in central laboratories by blinded
personnel unaware of the participants' randomization
assignment.
IP accountability and safety reporting
IP accountability was monitored by on‐site visits and
investigator diary completion. All IPs were packaged
centrally with prespecified amounts. The on‐site clinical
research associate would check returned IP from each
participant and cross‐reference the participant diary to
confirm study compliance. Two incidents of <80%
compliance resulted in the participant being removed
from the study by the investigator.
Safety information was gathered systematically by
the investigator team according to the specifications
contained within the study protocol. These data were
coded based on industry‐standard principles using
MedDRA. Preferred term coding was performed by a
medically qualified staff member.
Statistical analysis
Based on a hypothesized mean difference of 10.0 absolute
percentage points in HFF with an SD of 15.0 for the
treatment group (NRPT 2×) and 10.0 for placebo and an
estimated dropout rate of 20%, 35 participants per group
were estimated to provide 80.5% power to reject the null
hypothesis of no effect, with two‐tailed tests with alpha = 0.05.
Power calculations were performed using a two‐sample
unequal variance t test in NCSS PASS13 (Kaysville, UT).
For this early‐stage investigation, baseline values of
characteristics between study groups were compared
using an ANOVA. The absolute changes from baseline
in each study endpoint were compared between study
groups using an analysis of covariance (ANCOVA) with
treatment, site, sex (male/female), age group (< 45,
≥45 years old), BMI group (25–29.9, 30–34.9, 35–40),
statin use (yes, no), and diabetic condition (yes, no) as
fixed
effects
(tested
with
a
backward
elimination
method; removed from the model if the significant level
for any of these was >0.1) and with the baseline
endpoint value as a covariate. Using the same model,
the within‐group changes from baseline in each study
endpoint were also assessed for statistical significance.
In the post hoc analysis, stratified subgroup analyses
were conducted based on baseline HFF ( ≤27, >27) for
the primary study endpoint of change in HFF. When
analyzing
baseline,
Day
90,
and
Day
180
data,
repeated‐measures analysis was used, allowing subject
variability to be partitioned from the treatment and error
terms. In all cases data were checked to ensure normal
distribution.
As shown in Figure 1B, the intent‐to‐treat (ITT)
population (n = 111) was used to assess all safety
endpoints and the modified ITT (mITT, n = 94)
population for baseline characteristics because this
group
did
have
both
baseline
and
end‐of‐study
measurements
of
HFF.
The
PP
population
was
derived from the mITT group prior to unblinding of the
data and included 84 participants, having excluded five
participants who had their final MRI more than 7 days
beyond the allotted time after the last dose, four
participants who were noncompliant with study prod-
ucts, and 1 participant lost to follow‐up. All analyses
were performed in SAS, version 9.4 or higher (Cary,
NC). p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant for
all analyses. Data were reported as mean ± SD for
continuous variables.
RESULTS
Trial overview
In this early‐phase trial we evaluated the safety and
efficacy of NRPT, a combination of the synergistic natural
compounds nicotinamide riboside and pterostilbene, as a
treatment for NAFLD in a randomized, double‐blind,
placebo‐controlled human clinical study of 111 adults
over a 6‐month period (Figure 1A). All 111 participants
were analyzed in the ITT group for safety endpoints, 94
participants were analyzed in the mITT group, and 84
participants were analyzed in the PP group (Figure 1B).
The PP group was defined before unblinding of the data,
and participants in the mITT group were excluded from
the
PP
group
for
such
protocol
deviations
as
noncompliance with taking the study product (< 80%
taken) or failing to get the required MRI‐PDFF within the
predetermined window at the end of study. No patients
failed to appear for the final MRI due to safety or
methodological
issues.
All
primary
and
secondary
endpoint data were analyzed using both the mITT and
the
PP
populations.
Exploratory
analyses
were
conducted solely in the PP group.

HFF
The primary efficacy objective of our study was to
determine if supplementation with NRPT daily over a 6‐
month period would result in a change in liver fat content
and if there was a dose effect of NRPT on changes from
baseline in liver fat content compared to placebo. This
primary endpoint was not met in either the mITT (Table 2)
or the PP populations (Table S1), whether assessed by
MRI‐PDFF imaging of the liver or by determination of the
FLI. However, in the mITT population both the placebo
and NRPT 1× groups showed a significant within‐group
decrease in HFF at end of study (Table 2). For the PP
population,
all
three
groups
showed

as measured by MRI‐PDFF (Table S1). Neither the
NRPT 1× nor the NRPT 2× group displayed a significant
decrease in HFF compared to placebo in either pop-
ulation (Table 2; Table S1).
Unfortunately, the placebo group by chance had a
concentration

group. Thus, we applied post hoc exploratory analysis

significant decrease in HFF (4.2%), and this decrease
was statistically significant with respect to baseline
(p < 0.0001) and with respect to the placebo group
(p = 0.003) (Table 3). In fact, HFF in the placebo group
did not change during the study. For participants who
started with >27% HFF, only the placebo group
displayed a decrease in HFF over the course of the
study (7.2%), which was statistically significant over

Adverse events
Overall, 41 participants (36.9%) experienced 87 treat-

The primary endpoint of a change in liver fat over a 6‐
month period, whether measured by HFF or FLI, did not
show any significant between‐group differences in this
study. Unfortunately, randomization yielded a placebo
arm with a significantly higher number of individuals with
a very high baseline HFF and who showed a large HFF
reduction during the trial, which could have confounded
the analysis. Therefore, we also did a post hoc analysis
of subgroups by stratifying baseline HFF into two
groups (< 27% or > 27% fat content). In the analysis
of the population with <27% HFF at baseline, the NRPT
1× arm showed a significant reduction in both HFF and
FLI compared to baseline and placebo during the trial.

differences in HFF or FLI reductions during the trial, with
the exception of the placebo group showing a significant
decrease
in
HFF
with
respect
to
baseline.
This
subgroup analysis suggests that the placebo effect
observed in this study may have been driven by
participants starting with extremely high HFF who by
chance were disproportionately placed into the placebo
group (Table 3; Figure S1).

(Table 2).
Another secondary endpoint in the trial was the
levels of ALT and other liver enzymes in the blood
because they are markers of liver damage due to an
inflammatory
response
to
fat
accumulation.
We
observed a decrease in ALT in the NRPT 1× treatment
arm that did not quite reach significance compared to
placebo in the mITT population but showed significance
compared to placebo in the PP population both at the
end of the study and at an intermediate time point. This
decrease was consistent with the findings of an earlier
trial.[23] Reductions in the NRPT 1× group in the PP
population were also observed for two other liver
enzymes that were significant with respect to placebo
(GGT) or approached significance with respect to
placebo (AST).
The observed reduction in ALT of 31% for NRPT 1×
corresponds to an absolute reduction of 14.8 U/L. In a
study of subjects with a histopathologic diagnosis of
NASH, a 17‐U/L decrease in ALT was associated with a
clear improvement in the NAFLD activity score.[30]
Notably, 14 out of the 33 participants in the NRPT 1×
group in the current study showed a decrease in ALT of
approximately 17 U/L or greater.
As presaged by an earlier trial,[23] the NRPT 2× arm
did not show the same reduction of liver enzymes
observed in the NRPT 1× arm. It is possible that the 2×
dose creates a physiology in which the activities of the
SIRTs are no longer optimized. For example, NR
hydrolysis generates nicotinamide, which is a known
SIRT1 inhibitor and might counteract the effect of
elevated NAD+ levels. Another possibility is that the
higher dose of PT in the 2× arm exceeds peak efficacy.
As a possible precedent, a 15‐mg/kg dose of PT
increased SIRT1 expression in a rodent steatosis
model, while a 30‐mg/kg dose did not.[31] A third
possibility is that the trend of a lower baseline ALT
and AST in the NRPT 2× group masked the effects of
the intervention. This trend was absent for GGT, which
did show a significant reduction in the NRPT 2× group.
Finally, it is possible that the observed reduction in liver
enzymes in the NRPT 1× group was simply due to
chance.

in hepatic fat content was not met, this proof‐of‐concept

NRPT in the treatment of liver damage in NAFLD and
