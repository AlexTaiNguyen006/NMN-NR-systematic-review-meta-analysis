A B S T R A C T
Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) depletion contributes to mitochondrial dysfunction, inflammation, 
cognitive and physical decline with aging, partly driven by upregulated cluster of differentiation 38 (CD38) 
activity. This 60-day randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial evaluated LN22199, a polyphenol-rich 
blend of Punica granatum and Tagetes erecta, alone or with nicotinamide riboside (NR), in 140 healthy aging 
adults (age: 60.09 ± 4.20 years; male: female 59 %: 41 %). Participants received either a placebo, NR (500 mg), 
LN22199 (1000 mg), or LN22199 (500 mg) + NR (500 mg). Outcomes included NAD+ levels, CD38 activity, 
inflammatory biomarkers, cognitive function, physical performance, and quality-of-life measures. LN22199 
increased blood NAD+ levels by 26.48 %, NR by 22.69 %, and their combination by 31.76 % (all p < 0.001 vs. 
baseline). CD38 activity decreased significantly with LN22199 alone (17.98 % reduction, p = 0.0025 vs. baseline; 
p = 0.0344 vs. placebo), while NR alone showed minimal change. Serum growth/differentiation factor 15 (GDF- 
15) levels declined by 15.07 % (LN22199; p = 0.0001), 11.36 % (NR; p = 0.0033), and 17.83 % (combination; p 
< 0.0001) vs. baseline. The combination group achieved the greatest improvements in functional outcomes, 
including a 9.94 % increase in six-minute walk distance and a 16.45 % improvement in Mini-Mental State Ex­
amination 2 cognitive scores. All treatments were well tolerated with no serious adverse events reported. 
LN22199, combined with NR, enhances NAD+ homeostasis, mitigates inflammation, and improves cognitive and 
physical function, representing a promising strategy for healthy aging.
1. Introduction
Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD+) is a vital pyridine 
nucleotide coenzyme involved in fundamental cellular processes, 
including energy metabolism, redox reactions, DNA repair, and signal 
transduction (Covarrubias et al., 2021; Verdin, 2015). As an electron 
carrier, NAD+ facilitates glycolysis, tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, and 
oxidative phosphorylation, and serves as an essential substrate for 
NAD+-consuming enzymes such as sirtuins, poly(ADP-ribose) poly­
merases (PARPs), and cluster of differentiation 38 (CD38). These en­
zymes regulate vital biological functions such as genomic stability, 
mitochondrial function, and calcium signaling (Cant´o et al., 2015; 
Verdin, 2015).
Advancing age and many age-related pathologies are associated with 
a steady reduction in NAD+ levels. This reduction contributes to mito­
chondrial dysfunction, elevated oxidative stress, and impaired activities 
of NAD+-dependent metabolic enzymes. (Mills et al., 2016; Rajman 
et al., 2018). Three primary biosynthetic pathways regulate the main­
tenance of intracellular NAD+ pools: (i) de novo synthesis from trypto­
phan, (ii) the Preiss–Handler pathway from nicotinic acid (NA), and (iii) 
the salvage pathway from nicotinamide (NAM) and nicotinamide ribo­
side (NR). Among these, the salvage pathway predominates in 
mammalian cells and is critically dependent on nicotinamide phos­
phoribosyltransferase (NAMPT), the key rate-limiting enzyme that

Numerous preclinical studies have demonstrated that supplementa­
tion with NR enhances NAD+ biosynthesis, improves mitochondrial 
biogenesis, and reduces inflammatory responses in models of aging and 
metabolic dysfunction (Mills et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 
2020). Human clinical trials further support the role of NR in restoring 
NAD+ levels and improving vascular function, skeletal muscle meta­
bolism, and neuroprotection, thus offering a compelling strategy for 
promoting healthy aging and extending healthy life span (Martens et al., 
2018; Elhassan et al., 2019; Remie et al., 2020; Xue et al., 2022; Sharma 
et al., 2023; D¨olle & Tzoulis, 2025). However, declining NAD+ levels 
with advancing age are not solely due to impaired biosynthesis but are 
also driven by increased consumption of NAD+, particularly by CD38. 
CD38 is a multifunctional NADase upregulated in metabolic tissues, 
immune cells, and senescent cells during aging and chronic inflamma­
tion (Camacho-Pereira et al., 2016; Chini et al., 2020). It hydrolyses 
NAD+ into ADP-ribose and NAM, significantly contributing to the age- 
related depletion of NAD+. This poses a therapeutic limitation, as sup­
plementation with NAD+ precursors alone may be inadequate to over­
come accelerated NAD+ degradation that occurs with advancing age.
To overcome this limitation, combined strategies have been pro­
posed, involving the use of NAD+ precursors (such as nicotinamide 
mononucleotide, NMN, and NR) in conjunction with the inhibition of 
NAD+-consuming enzymes, including CD38. Preclinical models suggest 
that pharmacological CD38 inhibition or genetic deletion, in combina­
tion with NAD+ precursors, more effectively restore intracellular NAD+
levels and improve mitochondrial and metabolic function (Peclat et al., 
2022; Tarrag´o et al., 2018). Although synthetic NAD+ precursors offer 
promise, limited bioavailability, high costs, regulatory ambiguity, and 
potential long-term safety concerns impede their applications. As a 
result, attention is shifting toward natural and plant-derived compounds 
as safer and more sustainable alternatives for modulating NAD+ meta­
bolism. Natural polyphenols such as apigenin and pomegranate-derived 
ellagitannins have been shown to inhibit CD38 and support mitochon­
drial health (Escande et al., 2013; Ogura et al., 2020), suggesting a 
potential complementary approach. However, clinical evidence sup­
porting a dual-mode strategy that combines NAD+ precursor supple­
mentation with targeted CD38 modulation to optimize and sustain 
intracellular NAD+ availability remains limited. (Chini et al., 2020).
In this context, LN22199, a standardized and proprietary 
polyphenol-rich herbal formulation composed of extracts from Punica 
granatum (pomegranate) whole fruit and Tagetes erecta (marigold) 
flowers, was developed. Preclinical studies demonstrated that LN22199 
synergistically promoted NAD+ synthesis and inhibited CD38 activity in 
vitro. Immunoblot assays demonstrated that LN22199 increased the 
expression of Nicotinate phosphoribosyltransferase (NAPRT), NAMPT, 
and NMN adenylyltransferase (NMNAT) 1 and -3 in L6 rat skeletal 
myocytes (unpublished data). These observations suggested that 
LN22199 might help promote cellular NAD synthesis from NA and NAM, 
activating the Preiss-Handler and Salvage pathways. Moreover, it also 
helped reduce intracellular NAD breakdown via inhibiting CD38 
enzyme activity. Subsequently, an in vivo proof-of-concept study 
demonstrated that LN22199 supplementation significantly elevated 
blood and tissue NAD+ levels in aged Sprague Dawley rats (unpublished 
data). Given LN22199’s dual mechanism—enhancing NAD+ biosyn­
thesis and inhibiting CD38-mediated degradation, we hypothesized that 
LN22199 would produce greater improvements in NAD+ levels, anti- 
inflammatory responses, and functional health benefits as compared to 
NR alone. Moreover, combining LN22199 with NR was expected to 
deliver synergistic benefits, resulting in the highest gains in NAD+
restoration and age-related functional outcomes among all treatment 
groups.
Punica granatum has been used traditionally in Ayurveda and Unani 
medicine for cardiovascular, gastrointestinal, and general tonic pur­
poses. It is rich in ellagitannins (notably punicalagin), ellagic acid, an­
thocyanins, and other polyphenols, which demonstrate potent 
antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and anti-aging benefits (Jurenka, 
2008). These phytochemicals have been shown to enhance mitochon­
drial function, modulate sirtuin pathways, and facilitate DNA repair 
(Zhao C et al., 2016; Guo et al., 2021). Tagetes erecta, widely used in 
traditional medicine in Latin America and Asia, is a rich source of lutein, 
zeaxanthin, and quercetagetin; these exhibit potent antioxidant and 
anti-inflammatory activities. (Escande et al., 2013). T. erecta has also 
demonstrated hepatoprotective, neuroprotective, and ocular benefits, 
particularly in age-related macular degeneration (Delgado-Vargas & 
Paredes-L´opez, 2003).
This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical trial was 
designed to evaluate the effects of LN22199, NR, and their combination 
(LN22199 + NR) on NAD+ and its metabolites in blood and urine over a 
60-day duration in healthy aging individuals. This trial evaluated the 
efficacy of LN22199 alone and in combination with NR, while 
comprehensively assessing NAD+ metabolism, CD38 activity, and bio­
markers of systemic and neurological well-being. The study assessed the 
impact of these interventions on a panel of molecular biomarkers 
including, growth differentiation factor 15 (GDF-15), interleukin 6 (IL- 
6), tumor necrosis factor α (TNF-α), CD38, neurofilament light chain 
(NFL), and functional health metrics that include endurance and 
cardiorespiratory fitness, cognitive health and quality of life, providing a 
comprehensive evaluation of cellular resilience, systemic inflammation, 
and functional capacity.
2. Materials and methods
2.1. Study material
LN22199 (BluNADBooster) is a patent-pending proprietary compo­
sition containing 90 % (w/w) blend of Punica granatum fruit water 
extract and Tagetes erecta flower aqueous-ethanol extract in a 3:1 ratio. It 
is combined with 10 % (w/w) neutral excipients, including rice malto­
dextrin, and syloid. The final product is standardized to contain not less 
than 1.5 % punicalagins, and 4.0 % quercetagetin, serving as phyto­
chemical reference markers for P. granatum, and T. erecta, respectively. 
LN22199 was developed by Laila Nutra Private Limited, Vijayawada, 
Andhra Pradesh, India, and manufactured at its current Good 
Manufacturing Practices (cGMP)-certified production facility. This study 
utilized a batch of LN22199 (LPP22100194, produced in October 2022 
with a minimum shelf life of two years). The major nutrient contents of 
this batch are presented in Supplementary Table S1. This study was 
conducted between December 2023 and May 2024.
2.2. Phytoconstituents and extraction procedures
P. granatum dried fruit and T. erecta flower petals were obtained from 
Madakasiri, Andhra Pradesh, India. A qualified taxonomist identified 
and confirmed the raw plant materials. Preserved specimens were 
archived in the herbarium of Laila Nutra Private Limited R&D Center 
(Vijayawada, Andhra Pradesh, India), with the accession numbers 
LN6797 for P. granatum and LN6847 for T. erecta.
The dried raw materials of P. granatum fruit and T. erecta flower 
petals were processed individually. The dried P. granatum fruit raw 
material was pulverized to a coarse powder and extracted twice with 
reverse osmosis (RO) water at 80–85 ◦C under stirring. The extracts were 
combined, filtered, and concentrated under vacuum at 60–70 ◦C, fol­
lowed by drying in a vacuum tray dryer at 70–80 ◦C to obtain 
P. granatum extract as a brown-colored powder. The dried T. erecta 
flower petals were pulverized into a coarse powder and extracted thrice 
with hydro alcohol at 50–60 ◦C. The extracts were combined, filtered, 
and concentrated under vacuum at 50–60 ◦C to obtain the concentrate 
containing approximately 40–55 % total solids. The thick paste was 
subjected to further drying in a vacuum tray dryer at 70–80 ◦C to get 
T. erecta extract as a brown-colored powder. The extracts of P. granatum 
and T. erecta were blended along with excipients to obtain a

2.3. High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) analysis
Analysis of LN22199 was performed using a Waters High Perfor­
mance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) system equipped with a 
thermostat-controlled column oven compartment, autosampler, photo­
diode array detector, and Empower 3 software (Waters Corporation, 
Milford, MA). The sample preparation involves the extraction of the 
sample using dimethyl sulfoxide: water (50:50), followed by filtration 
using a 0.22 μm PVDF filter. The sample solution was analyzed using an 
Agilent, Eclipse Plus C18 3.5 μm, 100 × 4.6 mm column (Agilent 
Technologies, Santa Clara, CA).
A gradient elution system consists of solvent A (0.1 % v/v ortho­
phosphoric acid in water) and solvent B (acetonitrile) as mobile phase 
with a flow rate of 0.8 mL/min. The run started at sample injection with 
a mixture of 96 % A and 4 % B as initial eluent for 5 min. Then a linear 
gradient was used to reach 91 % A and 9 % B in 10 min, followed by a 
linear gradient to reach 70 % A and 30 % B in 10 min. Finally, an iso­
cratic run at 70 % A, 30 % B for 5 min. The column oven compartment 
was maintained at 27 ◦C. A typical HPLC chromatogram of LN22199 is 
depicted in Fig. 1. The chromatographic profile at 258 nm shows three 
peaks at 14.352, 18.564, and 25.130 min, which represent Punicalagin B 
& A, and Quercetagetin, respectively. The identification of these peaks 
was carried out using respective reference standards, viz. Punicalagin 
(Cat.# P0023) and Quercetagetin (Cat.#85,766) (Sigma-Aldrich Corp, 
St. Louis, MO).
2.4. Clinical study
2.4.1. Study protocol registration and ethics approval
This clinical trial was registered with the Clinical Trials Registry of 
India (CTRI) under the registration number CTRI/2023/10/058217. 
This investigation was conducted in Latha Super Specialities Hospital, 
Anu Hospitals and Premier Super Speciality Hospital- MGAKI in 
Vijayawada, Andhra Pradesh, India, and the protocol was approved by 
the institutional ethics committees with registration numbers: ECR/ 
1478/Inst/AP/2020 (Aug 28, 2023), ECR/1049/Inst/AP/2018/RR-21 
(Aug 28, 2023), and ECR/1347/Inst/AP/2020 (Aug 24, 2023), 
respectively.
Before enrolment, all participants received comprehensive verbal 
and written information detailing the study’s purpose, procedures, po­
tential risks, and benefits. Each participant provided written consent in 
accordance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki and 
the International Council for Harmonisation – Good Clinical Practice 
(ICH-GCP) guidelines (World Medical Association, 2013; International 
Council for Harmonisation (ICH), 2016).
2.4.2. Participant selection criteria
Healthy, ambulatory male and female subjects (aged 55–70 years; 
BMI: 24–29.9 kg/m2) were enrolled in the study. Subjects were 
considered healthy by the principal investigator based on their medical 
history and thorough clinical laboratory examinations conducted during 
the screening process. A complete set of inclusion-exclusion criteria is 
summarized (supplementary Table S2).
2.4.3. Sample size calculation and power analysis
A total of 140 participants were randomized in a 1:1:1:1 ratio across 
the four treatment arms. The sample size was determined to achieve 90 
% statistical power to detect a mean difference of 3.0 μM in NAD+ level 
changes from baseline between treatment groups, assuming a pooled 
standard deviation of 3.7 μM, based on prior findings (Huang, 2022). 
The calculation was conducted using a two-tailed independent t-test 
with a significance level (α) of 0.05 and 95 % confidence intervals. To 
accommodate an estimated dropout rate of 5 % (approximately two 
participants per group), the enrolment target was set at 35 subjects per 
group, ensuring robust statistical validity for efficacy comparisons.
2.4.4. Study design, randomization, and blinding
This 60-day randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled clinical 
trial was conducted from December 2023 to May 2024. Following the 
selection through inclusion-exclusion criteria, the recruited participants 
were randomized into one of the four groups: (i) Placebo, (ii) 
LN22199–1000 mg, (iii) NR-500 mg, and (iv) LN22199 (500 mg) + NR 
(500 mg) (Fig. 2). The randomization codes were generated by the SAS 
procedure PROC PLAN using block designs. Upon enrolment, each 
participant was assigned a unique sequential identification number. The 
randomization sequence was prepared by an independent statistician, 
who was not involved in participant recruitment or clinical assessments 
and maintained allocation concealment. The participants and

2.4.5. Intervention and follow-up
Each participant consumed two capsules daily, each containing 
either 500 mg of LN22199, 250 mg of NR, LN22199 (250 mg) + NR 
(250 mg), or a placebo, after breakfast, over a 60-day period. The 
investigational product (IP) capsule size “0” was supplied in coded white 
high-density polyethylene (HDPE) bottles. Each subject received one 
bottle containing sixty-four capsules of an IP during the enrolment 
(baseline) and the follow-up visit (day 30). Investigators (or designees) 
transcribed the subject number, subject initials, and date dispensed on 
the study medication label.
At follow-up (day 30) and final visits, adherence to the intervention 
was assessed through capsule count. Participants were considered non- 
adherent if more than 10 % of the capsules dispensed remained un­
used. General health, well-being, and the presence of any adverse events 
(AEs) were evaluated and recorded at every visit by the study in­
vestigators. The principal investigator (PI) conducted detailed physical 
examinations and safety monitoring to assess the clinical status and 
document any emergent or ongoing AEs. Each participant maintained a 
daily diary that recorded food consumption (via a 3-day recall), IP 
intake, and daily activities, which the study investigators duly endorsed 
at every visit. The study coordinator closely monitored the participants’ 
adherence to the study supplement and regularly verified their 
compliance with the study through daily diary records.
Overnight fasting blood samples were collected from the antecubital 
vein using standard aseptic venipuncture techniques by certified phle­
botomists. Samples obtained at screening and on day 60 were used for 
hematological and blood biochemistry and serum biomarkers evalua­
tions. Blood samples collected at baseline, days 30, and 60 of treatment 
were utilized in assessing NAD+ and its metabolites, and NADase (CD38) 
activity in the peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs). All sam­
pling procedures were conducted in accordance with Good Clinical 
Laboratory Practice (GCLP) standards to ensure the integrity, reliability, 
and traceability of the samples throughout the study.
2.4.6. Blood sample preparation and chromatographic analysis of NAD+
and its metabolites
Quantifications of NAD+ and its key metabolic intermediates- nico­

(NMN), nicotinic acid (NA), nicotinamide (NAM), and 1-methylnicoti­

chromatography-mass spectrometry/mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS)

For the determination of NAD+, NAAD, and NMN, freshly collected 
overnight fasting blood samples were stabilized immediately by dilution 
(1:2) with 1 % perchloric acid to preserve nucleotide integrity (Liang 
et al., 2014). For the determination of NA, NAM, and 1-MNAM, freshly 
collected blood samples were diluted (1:2) with ultrapure water before 
freezing at −80 ◦C (Trammell & Brenner, 2013).
The detailed methods of chromatographic identifications of NAD+, 
NAAD, NMN, and NA, NAM, 1-MNAM are described in the supple­

2.4.8. CD38 (NADase) assay
Venous blood (9 mL) was drawn from each subject using BD Vacu­
tainer® K2E (EDTA) tubes (BD Vacutainer Systems, Plymouth, UK) 
under standard aseptic conditions. PBMCs were isolated using Lym­
phoprep™ density gradient medium (Cat# 07801, Stem-cell Technolo­
gies, Vancouver, Canada), following the standard method (Tran et al., 
2016). Isolated PBMCs were washed, resuspended in freezing medium, 
and cryopreserved in liquid nitrogen until further analysis. For CD38 
enzymatic activity assessment, cryopreserved PBMCs were resuspended 
in RPMI-1640 medium supplemented with 10 % fetal bovine serum 
(FBS) and incubated overnight in a humidified CO₂ incubator at 37 ◦C 
and 5 % CO₂. The following day, cells were harvested and lysed in su­
crose buffer (40 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.4, containing 0.25 M sucrose and 1 
% Triton X-100), followed by brief sonication. The cell lysates were 
clarified at 18,928 ×g for 10 min at room temperature (RT), and the 
protein content was estimated using the Pierce BCA Protein Assay Kit 
(Cat# 23225, Thermo Scientific, Waltham, MA, USA) according to the 
manufacturer’s protocol.
The CD38 enzymatic activity was measured using a fluorometric 
assay with Ɛ-NAD (Cat# N2630, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, MO) as the 
substrate (Chini et al., 2020). The reaction mixture (200 μL) consisted of 
25 μg of total protein in sucrose buffer, combined with 100 μL of Ɛ-NAD 
(50 μM) solution. The mixture was gently vortexed (20 s) and incubated 
for 1 h at RT in the dark. Endpoint fluorescence was recorded using a 
multimode fluorescence microplate reader (EnSpire™, PerkinElmer 
Inc., Shelton, CT) at an excitation/emission wavelength of 290/400 nm. 
The CD38 activity was expressed as relative fluorescence units (RFU)/ 
mg protein.
2.4.9. Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA)
Serum biomarker levels were evaluated using validated, commer­
cially available ELISA kits, following the manufacturers’ protocols. The 
biomarker concentrations were measured using the Neurofilament light 
chain (NFL) ELISA kit (Cat# abx152468; Abbexa, Cambridge, UK). The 
interleukin-6 (IL-6, Cat# HS600C), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α, 
Cat# HSTA00E), and growth differentiation factor 15 (GDF-15, Cat# 
DGD150) were quantified using Human Quantikine ELISA kits procured 
from R&D Systems (Minneapolis, MN). The lower limits of detection 
(LLD) for NFL, IL-6, TNF-α, and GDF-15 assays were 5.70 pg/mL, 0.09 
pg/mL, 0.049 pg/mL, and 4.39 pg/mL, respectively.
2.4.10. Six-minute walk test (6MWT)
The Six-Minute Walk Test (6MWT) is a validated submaximal exer­
cise assessment that measures the distance an individual can walk in six 
minutes on a flat, hard surface. It is commonly used to evaluate func­
tional capacity, endurance, and cardiorespiratory fitness (ATS Com­
mittee on Proficiency Standards for Clinical Pulmonary Function 
Laboratories, 2002). Participants are instructed to walk back and forth 
along a 30-m corridor at their own pace, with standardized encour­
agement provided at regular intervals. The total distance walked in 
meters is recorded.
2.4.11. Mini-mental state examination 2– Standard version (MMSE-2 SV)
The MMSE-2 standard version (SV) is a standardized and updated 
version of the original Mini-Mental State Examination, used for brief 
screening of cognitive impairment. The standard version provides a 
quick and reliable assessment tool suitable for longitudinal tracking of 
cognitive changes in clinical and research settings (Folstein et al., 1975).
2.4.12. Quality of life questionnaire (SF-36)
The Short Form-36 (SF-36) is a widely used, validated questionnaire 
designed to as-sess health-related quality of life (HRQoL) across eight 
domains: (i) physical functioning, (ii) role limitations due to physical or 
(iii) emotional problems, (iv) bodily pain, (v) general health, (vi) vi­
tality, (vii) social functioning, and (viii) emotional well-being (Ware Jr. 
& Sherbourne, 1992). The questionnaire comprises 36 questions scored 
on Likert scales, with domain-specific scores calculated and aggregated 
to provide two summary measures, the Physical Component Summary 
(PCS) and the Mental Component Summary (MCS).
2.4.13. Newcastle mitochondrial quality of life measure (NMQ)
The Newcastle Mitochondrial Quality of Life Measure (NMQ) is a 
disease-specific tool designed to evaluate the impact of mitochondrial 
disease on a patient’s quality of life (Elson et al., 2013). The instrument 
includes multiple items addressing (i) fatigue, (ii) emotional well-being, 
(iii) physical limitations, and (iv) social function. Scores are standard­
ized, and domain-specific, as well as total scores are computed.
2.5. Safety assessment and adverse events
The safety of LN22199, NR, or LN22199 + NR supplementation was 
evaluated through systematic monitoring of adverse events (AEs), vital 
signs, hematology, serum biochemistry, and urinalysis. AEs were 
documented by severity and causality throughout the study. Vital signs, 
including blood pressure, heart rate, respiratory rate, and temperature, 
were assessed at screening, baseline, and days 30 and 60 of the study.
Hematological tests included hemoglobin, RBC, WBC, platelet 
counts, and differential leukocyte analysis. Serum biochemistry evalu­
ated fasting glucose, blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, uric acid, 
albumin, total cholesterol, low-density lipoprotein (LDL), high-density 
lipoprotein (HDL), very low-density lipoprotein (VLDL), triglycerides, 
bilirubin, aspartate transaminase (AST), alanine transaminase (ALT), 
alkaline phosphatase (ALP), creatine kinase (CK), sodium, and potas­
sium. Urinalysis assessed the color, pH, specific gravity, protein, 
glucose, and the presence of RBCs. All laboratory analyses were per­
formed using standardized protocols following Good Laboratory Prac­
tice (GLP) guidelines.
2.6. Statistical analysis
Per-protocol (PP) analyses were conducted on data from 137 par­
ticipants who completed all study visits and adhered to the intervention 
protocol. Baseline demographic variables and within-group changes in 
primary and secondary efficacy endpoints were analyzed using paired 
two-sample t-tests. Safety analyses included all participants who 
received at least one dose of the study supplement (safety population), 
with between-group comparisons for hematological and biochemical 
parameters performed using independent t-tests.
For intergroup comparisons of efficacy outcomes, Analysis of 
Covariance (ANCOVA) was employed, adjusting for baseline values as 
covariates. Post-hoc pairwise comparisons were corrected using the 
Bonferroni method to control Type I error. All statistical tests were two- 
tailed, with a significance threshold set at p < 0.05. Statistical analyses 
were performed using SAS software version 9.4 (SAS Institute Inc., Cary, 
NC).

considered lost to follow-up. The efficacy analyses were performed on 
the per-protocol (PP) population, comprising placebo (n = 34), 
LN22199–1000 mg (n = 34), NR-500 mg (n = 34), and LN22199 (500 
mg) + NR (500 mg) (n = 35). Overall, based on the IP capsule con­
sumption record, the group-wise mean IP compliances were 98.47 %, 
97.99 %, 97.89 % and 97.82 % in the placebo, LN22199, NR, and the 
combined treatment groups, respectively. The participant flow, from 
recruitment to study completion, is summarized in a CONSORT diagram 
(Fig. 2).
3.2. Blood NAD+ and metabolite levels
The baseline NAD+ levels were similar across groups, ranging be­
tween 24.7 and 26.0 μM. The placebo group showed no significant 
changes as compared to baseline at either day 30 or day 60. In contrast, 
all treatment groups demonstrated significant increases from baseline. 
LN22199–1000 increased NAD+ by 19.76 % (day 30) and 26.48 % (day 
60; p < 0.0001 vs. baseline); these changes are significant vs. placebo 
(Table 2). On days 30 and 60, the NR-500 group showed increases of 
9.62 % (p = 0.0109) and 22.69 % (p < 0.0001) from baseline; the change 
on day 60 was significantly different from that of the placebo group (p =
0.0009). The combination group (LN22199–500 + NR-500) yielded 
greater increases (23.14 % and 31.76 % on day 30 and 60 of treatment, 
respectively, p < 0.0001 vs. baseline; these increases are significant as 
compared to placebo (p = 0.0005 and p < 0.0001, respectively). How­
ever, these changes were not significant compared to either the 
LN22199 or NR groups (Table 2). The nicotinamide mononucleotide 
(NMN) levels in the LN22199–1000 mg group exhibited substantial in­
creases of 10.53 % (day 30, p = 0.0044) and 15.79 % (day 60, p <
0.0001), as compared to baseline, with a significant difference vs. pla­
cebo at the end of the study (p = 0.0124). Similarly, NR-500 supple­
mentation yielded a substantial increase on day 60 (15.79 %; p < 0.0001 
vs. baseline; p = 0.0087 vs. placebo). The combined treatment group 
showed increases of 10.53 % (p = 0.0039) and 21.05 % (p < 0.0001) on 
days 30 and 60, respectively, with a significant improvement over the 
placebo on day 60 (p = 0.0023). In the placebo group, on day 30, a 5 % 
increase in NMN levels as compared to baseline was marginal but sta­
tistically significant (p = 0.0222) (Table 2).
The nicotinamide (NAM) levels in the placebo group showed modest 
increases at day 30 (3.76 %, p = 0.0630) and day 60 of treatment (4.05 
%, p = 0.1566) as compared to baseline. The LN22199–1000 mg group 
increased NAM by 13.13 % (day 30; p = 0.0008) and 15.52 % (day 60; p 
= 0.0010), while NR-500 showed similar gains (11.61 %, p = 0.0023 and 
13.69 %, p = 0.0031; vs. baseline). The combination group resulted in 
slightly greater increases (14.94 %, p = 0.0015 and 17.68 %, p = 0.0008 
on days 30 and 60, respectively, compared to baseline). However, these 
increases were not significantly greater than those to either the LN22199 
or NR supplemented groups (Table 2).
The values of NAAD, NR, and 1-MNAM were below the lower limits 
of quantification (LLOQ) of the analytical methods (data not presented). 
The LLOQs of NAAD, NR, and 1-MNAM were 46.08, 46.08, and 25.92 
ng/mL, respectively.
3.3. Urinary metabolites and markers
A comprehensive analysis of urinary metabolites demonstrated sig­
nificant modulation of NAD+ catabolism and methylation-associated 
markers in response to supplementation with LN22199, NR, and their 
combination over 60 consecutive days (Table 3).
3.4. 1-methyl nicotinamide (1-MNAM)
Significant increases in urinary 1-MNAM levels were observed in all 
active groups after 30 and 60 days of supplementation. As compared to 
baseline, the NR group showed the highest increase (38.71 %, p =
0.0025; 51.61 %, p < 0.0001), followed by LN22199 + NR (25.00 %, p 
= 0.0153; 46.88 %, p < 0.0001) and LN22199 (25.81 %, p = 0.0290; 
45.16 %, p = 0.0007) on day 30 and 60, respectively. At the end of the 
study, all treatment groups were significantly different from the placebo, 
but not from each other (Table 3).
3.5. N-methyl-2-pyridone-5-carboxamide (2-Py)
NR and LN22199 + NR groups showed significant increases in 2-Py 
excretion on day 60 (39.36 %, p = 0.0163; 30.00 %, p = 0.0174, vs. 
baseline). The combined treatment group also increased 2-Py excretion 
by 23 % (p = 0.0346 vs. baseline) following 30 days of supplementation. 
The LN22199 group did not exhibit significant increases in urinary 2-Py 
(vs. baseline). Compared to the placebo group, the treatment groups 
showed trends of elevation; however, between-group comparisons were 
not statistically significant (Table 3).
3.6. Nicotinamide (NAM)
On day 60, the NR and LN22199 + NR groups showed significant 
increases in NAM compared to baseline (p < 0.0001 and p = 0.0011, 
respectively) and placebo (p = 0.0089 and p = 0.0492, respectively). 
The placebo and LN22199 groups did not show significant changes in 
the NAM levels during the intervention. However, on day 60, the 
intergroup comparison between LN22199 and NR groups was significant 
(p = 0.0395) (Table 3).
3.7. Methionine and homocysteine
No significant intragroup or intergroup changes in the urinary 
methionine or homocysteine levels were detected in any group 
throughout the study period (Table 3).

biomarkers in the study participants. The biomarkers included CD38

growth differentiation factor 15 (GDF-15), neurofilament light chain 
(NFL), tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α), and interleukin-6 (IL-6).
3.9. CD38 activity
Post-trial, CD38 activity was significantly reduced in the LN22199- 
supplemented group compared to baseline (17.98 %, p = 0.0025) and 
was also substantially lower than in the placebo group (p = 0.0344). On 
day 30, the LN22199 group also showed a significant reduction in CD38 
activity compared to baseline (12.36 %, p = 0.0296) but not compared 
to the placebo group (p = 0.1475). At the end of the study, the NR alone 
and LN22199 + NR combination groups showed smaller, non-significant 
reductions in CD38 activity (3.27 % and 9.74 % vs. baseline, respec­
tively) (Table 4).
3.10. Growth differentiation factor-15 (GDF-15)
Following 60 days of supplementation, GDF-15, a biomarker of 
cellular stress and aging, significantly decreased (vs. baseline) in all 
active groups: LN22199 (15.07 %, p = 0.0001), NR (11.36 %, p =
0.0033), and LN22199 + NR (17.83 %, p < 0.0001). The changes are 
also significant compared to placebo (Table 4).
3.11. Neurofilament light chain (NFL)
Post-trial, NFL, a marker for axonal damage and neurodegeneration, 
was significantly reduced in the LN22199 + NR group (14.07 %, p <
0.0001 vs. baseline) and in the LN22199 group (8.92 %, p = 0.0011 vs. 
baseline); these changes are also significant as compared to placebo (p =
0.0009 and p = 0.0425, respectively. NR supplementation reduced the 
NFL levels by 5.20 % (p = 0.0609). However, on day 60, an intergroup 
comparison revealed a significant reduction in the combination group 
(p = 0.0197), compared to the NR-500 mg-supplemented group 
(Table 4).
3.12. TNF-α and IL-6
Levels of the proinflammatory cytokine TNF-α were significantly 
reduced (vs. baseline) in all three active arms, with the greatest reduc­
tion observed in the LN22199 + NR group by 17.31 % (p = 0.0005). 
followed by the NR (13.46 %, p = 0.0202) and LN22199 (12.96 %, p =
0.0234) groups. On day 60, the LN22199 + NR group showed a signif­
icant reduction compared to placebo (p = 0.0081) (Table 4).
Similarly, post-trial serum IL-6 levels were also reduced in the 
LN22199 (15.09 %, p = 0.0121), NR (18.87 %, p = 0.0029), and 
LN22199 + NR (20.37 %, p = 0.0010) groups, compared to baseline. 
However, intergroup comparison analyses revealed that these changes 
were not significant compared to the placebo or between the active 
groups (Table 4).
3.13. Six-minute walk distance (6MWD)
At baseline, the 6-min walk distance (6MWD) in the study groups 
was comparable. Following 60 days of supplementation, LN22199, NR, 
and the combination group showed increases of 6.85 %, 6.59 %, and 
9.94 % in 6MWD, respectively. These changes were significantly greater 
(p < 0.0001) than those at baseline and placebo. Post-trial comparisons 
between the combination group and LN22199 (p = 0.0013) and the NR 
group (p = 0.0001) were significant (Table 5).
3.14. Mini-mental state examination (MMSE-2 SV)
Cognitive performance, as assessed by the MMSE-2 SV, improved 
significantly across all active treatment groups. Following 60 days of 
supplementation, the LN22199 + NR group exhibited the greatest 
enhancement (16.45 %, p < 0.0001 vs. baseline and placebo), followed 
by 11.74 % and 6.41 % increases (p < 0.0001 vs. baseline) in the 
LN22199 and NR groups, respectively, which were also statistically 
superior vs. placebo. Additionally, the combination group showed 
significantly greater increases as compared to either the LN22199 (p =
0.0015) or the NR group (p < 0.0001). The placebo group showed 
negligible changes (Supplementary Table S3).
3.15. Health-related and mitochondrial quality of life measures
All active treatment groups demonstrated significant improvements 
in health-related quality of life (HRQoL) across multiple SF-36 domains 
compared to baseline and the placebo (Supplementary Table S4). The 
LN22199 + NR combination group demonstrated substantial (p <
0.0001 vs. placebo) increases on day 60, especially in physical func­
tioning, vitality/energy, and bodily pain. At the end of the trial, this 
group also showed significant improvements (compared to the placebo) 
in emotional well-being, social functioning, and general health percep­
tion scores. LN22199 and NR groups showed moderate but statistically 
significant improvements from baseline in physical functioning, 
emotional role limitations, bodily pain, vitality, emotional well-being, 
social functioning, and general health (Supplementary Table S4).
Across all New castle Mitochondrial Quality of Life Questionnaire 
(NMQ) domains, participants receiving LN22199 + NR showed the 
greatest improvements on day 60, with statistically significant im­
provements in mobility (p < 0.0001), energy/fatigue (p < 0.0001), 
vision (p = 0.0008), and memory/cognition (p < 0.0001), all superior to 
placebo, NR, or LN22199-supplemented groups. However, the NR or 
LN22199 supplementations also showed significant improvements 
across various domains, particularly in energy/fatigue, daily living, 
stigma, and pain. The combined treatment consistently showed greater 
benefits (Supplementary Table S5).

4. Discussion
The present 60-day randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled 
study demonstrates that LN22199, a standardized polyphenol-rich 
phytoceutical formulation comprising P. granatum and T. erecta, alone 
or in combination with nicotinamide riboside (NR), significantly en­
hances NAD+ metabolism, decreases CD38 activity, reduces systemic 
inflammation, and improves cognitive and physical functions in healthy 
aging individuals. These findings add translational value to emerging 
preclinical data and highlight the synergistic potential of plant-derived 
CD38 inhibitors with NAD+ precursors (Alam et al., 2021; Liu et al., 
2024; Martel et al., 2019).
One interesting finding in this proof-of-concept study is that sup­
plementing the phytoceutical composition LN22199 over 60 days 
significantly increased blood NAD+ levels in the participants. Also, 
LN22199 supplementation significantly reduced CD38 activity and 
increased intracellular NMN and NAM levels, key intermediates in 
NAD+ metabolism. These changes suggest a conservation of intermedi­
ate molecules/precursors to NAD+ via a possible enhancement of the 
salvage pathway. These observations support unpublished preclinical 
findings of increased NAD+ levels in L6 rat skeletal myocytes and 
LN22199-supplemented aged Sprague Dawley rats, as well as the inhi­
bition of CD38 (unpublished data).
This study reaffirms prior observations that NR supplementation 
significantly raises NAD+ levels by providing substrates for the salvage 
pathway (Rajman et al., 2018; Trammell et al., 2016). The combination 
group, although not statistically superior to LN22199 alone in terms of 
CD38 inhibition, showed enhanced NAD+ levels and improved func­
tional outcomes, indicating synergistic biochemical and physiological 
benefits. Importantly, the increase in NAD levels was potentiated in the 
combined group, suggesting a superior approach that comprises 
enhanced biosynthesis and protection of NAD+ via CD38 inhibition. This 
observation supports the hypothesis of a combined strategy involving 
enhanced biosynthesis and reduced degradation, leading to superior 
homeostasis of NAD+ and greater physiological benefits (Peclat et al., 
2022; Tarrag´o et al., 2018).
Corroborating the systemic findings, urinary metabolite profiling 
revealed that LN22199, both alone and in combination, increased uri­
nary excretion of NAM, indicative of increased NAD+ turnover and 
preservation of intracellular NAD+ pools, consistent with CD38 inhibi­
tion. Conversely, elevated 1-MNAM levels in NR and combination 
groups indicate enhanced NAM methylation, a detoxification pathway 
for excess NAD+ metabolites (Rajman et al., 2018; Trammell et al., 
2016). The combination group displayed elevated blood NMN with low 
renal excretion, suggesting efficient cellular uptake and utilization. 
These observations suggest a possible mechanistic insight that LN22199 
decreases catabolism, NR serves as an NAD precursor, and together they 
promote enhanced NAD+ conservation and recycling, contributing to 
increased NAD+ levels and improved functional benefits.
LN22199 represents a novel approach by leveraging the synergistic 
actions of standardized phytonutrients. Phytoconstituents in LN22199, 
including punicalagins, ellagic acid, and quercetagetin, exert distinctive 
and convergent roles in mitochondrial regulation and NAD+ preserva­
tion (Anchimowicz et al., 2025). Punicalagins and ellagic acid upregu­
late SIRT1 and PGC-1α, key regulators of mitochondrial biogenesis and 
DNA repair (Guo et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2016). On the other hand, 
quercetagetin has been shown to inhibit CD38, thereby maintaining 
NAD+ pools and promoting sirtuin activation (Delgado-Vargas & Par­
edes-L´opez, 2003; Escande et al., 2013; Ogura et al., 2020). The present 
findings align with broader evidence that polyphenol-mediated CD38 
inhibition can restore NAD+ under oxidative stress in hepatic and renal 
systems (Arabshomali et al., 2023; Escande et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2024). 
The effect of LN22199 supplementation on intracellular NAM and NMN 
further suggests an enhancement of the salvage pathway efficiency and 
reduced NAD+ consumption via CD38 inhibition.
Elevated proinflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α and IL-6 are 
hallmarks of inflammaging and may contribute to cognitive and physical 
decline during aging (Franceschi et al., 2018). The reductions in TNF-α 
and IL-6 across all active groups suggest that both NR and LN22199 
exert systemic anti-inflammatory effects. The reductions were most 
pronounced in the combination group, highlighting the importance of 
modulating both biosynthetic and catabolic pathways of NAD+ meta­
bolism. NFL, a sensitive biomarker of neuronal damage, was signifi­
cantly reduced only in the LN22199 and combination groups, suggesting 
that polyphenols have neuroprotective effects beyond NR. Ellagitannins 
from P. granatum have been reported to inhibit microglial activation, 
reduce oxidative stress, and preserve blood-brain barrier integrity 
(Ahmedy et al., 2022; Jurenka, 2008; Wang et al., 2020). Similarly, 
apigenin has been shown to have neurotrophic effects and sirtuin acti­
vation in neural tissue (Bijani et al., 2022). The significant decline in 
GDF-15, a biomarker associated with mitochondrial dysfunction and 
cellular stress, further supports the hypothesis that LN22199 and NR 
enhance mitochondrial resilience (di Candia et al., 2021). The additive 
effect observed in the LN22199 + NR group is consistent with preclinical 
findings where CD38 inhibition restored NAD+ and improved mito­
chondrial bioenergetics (Camacho-Pereira et al., 2016).
The improvements observed in 6MWD, MMSE-2 SV scores, and SF-36 
domains substantiate the metabolic and anti-inflammatory benefits at 
the functional level. These changes are consistent with previous findings 
reporting NR-mediated improvements in endurance and muscle mito­
chondrial function (Martens et al., 2018) and plant polyphenol-induced 
improvements in cognitive function and quality of life (Cheng et al., 
2022). The superior outcomes in the LN22199 + NR group, especially in 
vitality, physical functioning, and memory/cognition, highlight the 
therapeutic relevance of combining precursor loading with enzymatic 
protection. NR primarily targets biosynthetic insufficiency by enhancing

This proof-of-concept investigation involved a relatively homoge­
neous and healthy population, which may limit generalizability to other

Importantly, the safety data analysis suggests that LN22199, alone 
and in combination with NR, is safe and well-tolerated, with no clinical 
evidence of organ toxicity or adverse events. This safety profile, along 
with the observed physiological benefits, supports the potential use of 
LN22199 as a nutritional strategy to promote healthy aging.
5. Conclusion
This randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study demon­
strates that LN22199, a standardized polyphenol-rich phytoceutical 
formulation, significantly enhances NAD+ levels, inhibits CD38 activity, 
reduces inflammatory and neurodegenerative markers, and improves 
physical and cognitive function in healthy aging individuals. When 
combined with NR, the effects were enhanced, achieving a superior 
NAD+ restoration and functional gains in the participants. These find­
ings support a dual-action strategy of boosting NAD+ biosynthesis and 
limiting its degradation as a promising approach to mitigate advancing 
age-associated decline. LN22199, when used alone or in combination 
with NR, provides a comprehensive intervention strategy that emerges 
as a safe and effective nutraceutical approach to promote healthy aging 
and mitochondrial function.
