# Response to Reviewers

## Editor Comments
*Comment 1*: The revised manuscript should more clearly justify the validity of the indirect comparison in light of the substantial differences between the NMN and NR evidence bases. The discussion of transitivity should be strengthened, and the conclusions should be reframed to emphasise the limitations of the available evidence rather than implying comparative efficacy or equivalence between the two interventions. The interpretation of the findings should consistently reflect the very low certainty of the evidence.
*Response*: We thank the editor for this critical feedback. We have reframed the manuscript throughout to emphasize that this study serves to map the evidence gap and feasibility assessment, and to highlight the structural lack of transitivity. We have revised our language to clarify that current evidence is insufficient to establish meaningful differences between NMN and NR, rather than implying equivalence. We have explicitly reinforced the "very low certainty" of the evidence in the abstract and conclusions.

*Comment 2*: The statistical methodology also requires further clarification, particularly regarding the choice of random effects estimator, the handling of sparse data, crossover trials, multiplicity adjustments, and the robustness of the findings through appropriate sensitivity analyses where feasible. Greater transparency regarding protocol chronology, outcome harmonisation, and data handling would further strengthen the methodological rigour of the review.
*Response*: We have expanded the Methods and Results sections to transparently detail our rationale for statistical modeling (e.g., justifying DerSimonian-Laird and noting REML validation), the handling of crossover trials (e.g., pre-washout vs separate parallel treatment approaches), our prespecified vs. post hoc multiplicity adjustments, and comprehensive outcome harmonization. Sensitivity findings and alternative estimators are now explicitly discussed.

*Comment 3*: Both reviewers also recommend improving the discussion by providing a stronger biological context for the observed findings, including mechanistic differences between NMN and NR, potential dose-response implications, and the clinical relevance of the identified evidence gaps. The manuscript would additionally benefit from improved conciseness, reduced repetition, consistent terminology, and careful attention to presentation throughout.
*Response*: We have revised the Discussion to provide a stronger biological context, contrasting the cellular uptake profiles (e.g. Slc12a8 vs. CD38 mechanisms) and addressing the dose differences. The manuscript has been heavily edited for conciseness, and terminology has been standardized across all sections and tables.

## Reviewer 1
*Comment 1*: Fundamental concerns regarding transitivity and validity of indirect comparison... The authors should address this issue more explicitly and consider reframing the manuscript away from an emphasis on comparative efficacy between NMN and NR toward mapping the evidence gap with exploratory indirect comparison. 
*Response*: We fully agree with this assessment. We have reframed our objectives and conclusions to focus on identifying structural asymmetry and evidence gaps rather than conclusive comparative efficacy. We emphasize that indirect estimates are strictly exploratory.

*Comment 2*: Overinterpretation of null findings. The manuscript would benefit from more cautious interpretation of null findings... "Current evidence is insufficient to establish meaningful differences between NMN and NR," rather than wording that may imply no meaningful difference exists.
*Response*: Thank you for this excellent suggestion. We have revised all mentions of null findings to accurately reflect that "current evidence is insufficient to establish meaningful differences". 

*Comment 3*: Statistical methodology requires stronger justification. The authors should therefore clarify the rationale for selecting DL as the primary estimator, indicate whether Hartung–Knapp adjustments were considered, and explicitly state whether REML materially altered conclusions.
*Response*: We have provided clearer justification for our statistical approaches. We added language to the Statistical Analysis section explaining our use of REML and Hartung-Knapp as sensitivity checks, clarifying that they did not materially alter confidence intervals or conclusions in this sparse evidence base. We explicitly acknowledge the instability of DL with very few trials.

*Comment 4*: Excessive reliance on single-study outcomes. Stronger caveats regarding interpretability are needed.
*Response*: We now explicitly flag all outcomes relying on single studies, emphasizing that these do not represent a pooled meta-analytic effect. We softened the language around these single-study comparisons throughout.

*Comment 5*: Clinical heterogeneity may be too extensive for pooled interpretation. Disease-specific populations may have distinct metabolic pathways... clarify whether subgroup analyses by metabolic health status were considered.
*Response*: We acknowledge the substantial heterogeneity between NR's disease-specific cohorts and NMN's generally healthy cohorts. We added discussion elaborating on how these different baseline conditions inherently weaken transitivity and explain why further subgroup analyses were statistically unfeasible but conceptually necessary in future research.

*Comment 6*: Clarify handling of crossover studies. Greater detail is needed regarding within-subject correlation, carryover effects, washout periods.
*Response*: We revised the Methods to explicitly outline how we handled crossover designs, detailing the assumptions we used regarding within-subject variance and correlation, the length of washouts evaluated, and how we primarily treated first-period data to avoid carryover effects where feasible. 

*Comment 7*: Multiplicity adjustment needs stronger rationale. Explicitly justify Bonferroni/Benjamini-Hochberg...
*Response*: We clarified in the text that multiple comparisons corrections were exploratory and not strictly powered due to study size. We explicitly reported that adjustment did not alter any primary inferences (since unadjusted estimates were already non-significant) and helped control family-wise error rate in generating exploratory hypotheses.

*Comment 8*: Discussion should better contextualise biological plausibility. More detailed discussion of the mechanistic differences between NMN and NR metabolism, distinct positions in salvage pathway... uncertainty surrounding Slc12a8.
*Response*: We developed a deeper mechanistic section in the Discussion highlighting the different routes of entry, reviewing the controversy around Slc12a8 expression in humans and interpreting how the molar dosing disparities complicate equivalent biological extrapolation.

*Comment 9*: Presentation and structure. Reduce repetition... improve conciseness. Standardize terminology (HbA1c). Clarify endpoint vs change values. Justify 2018. Add brief paragraph on regulatory uncertainty.
*Response*: We have removed repetitive content, standardized terms (e.g., using "glycated haemoglobin (HbA1c)" consistently), clarified that change-from-baseline extraction was prioritized, addressed the 2018 chronological search rationale based on when trials began publishing, and added a brief context on the regulatory landscape affecting NMN vs NR market dynamics.

## Reviewer 2
*Comment 1*: Registration and protocol chronology require clearer explanation.
*Response*: We apologize for the ambiguous phrasing. The "January 2018 to May 2025" timeframe referred strictly to the publication date filter applied to the databases, not the date the physical search was executed. We executed the database search, deduplication, and screening sequentially *after* our March 2026 PROSPERO registration. We have updated the Abstract and Methods section to make this timeline unambiguous, and we explicitly confirm that no eligibility criteria, outcomes, or analytic choices were altered after prospective registration.

*Comment 2*: Transitivity assessment... should avoid presenting the indirect estimates as comparative efficacy results.
*Response*: We have reframed the discussion of the Bucher findings exactly along these lines—the lack of transitivity structurally undermines comparative efficacy claims.

*Comment 3*: Statistical modeling requires more careful justification (DL vs REML, sparse evidence).
*Response*: As also requested by Reviewer 1, we expanded our statistical rationale, explicitly noting that estimation uncertainty—rather than strict hypothesis testing—is the appropriate frame for such sparse datasets, and clarified our REML validations.

*Comment 4*: Outcome harmonization needs additional transparency. A supplementary harmonization table should show...
*Response*: We agree and have created a detailed Supplementary Harmonization Table (Table S11) outlining exactly how endpoints, extraction metrics, and crossover handlings were standardized for metabolic outcomes.

*Comment 5*: Clinical and public-health conclusion should be moderated. Phrase as "comparative evidence is insufficient" rather than implying equivalence. 
*Response*: We have thoroughly adopted this change, including in the abstract and conclusion sections.

*Comment 6*: Cite additional recent literature (PMID: 41482231, PMID: 41935442, PMID: 37348156, PMID: 39595078) to improve contextualization.
*Response*: We have carefully integrated these four references into the Discussion and Introduction, improving the biological and contextual depth regarding metabolic oxidative stress, mitochondrial dysfunction, AMPK-linked processes, and dietary intervention evidence synthesis.

*Comment 7*: Standardize terminology (NMA, very low certainty, units etc). 
*Response*: We have scoured the text to correct "NMA" to "indirect comparison meta-analysis", ensured all GRADE ratings consistently read "very low certainty", and standardized all units (e.g., mg/dL, mmol/L) according to best practices.
